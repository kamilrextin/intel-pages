The 42 Lead Scoring Model

A two-axis framework for accurate B2B lead prioritization

The Core Principle

Most lead scoring fails because it over-indexes on engagement (clicks, downloads) while ignoring fit. A marketing intern who downloads every whitepaper shouldn't outscore a VP who visited your pricing page once.

The 42 Model splits scoring 50/50: 50 points for ICP fit, 50 points for engagement. Both matter equally.

1 ICP Fit Score (50 points)

Who they are. Firmographic attributes that indicate ideal customer match.

  • Company size0-15 pts
  • Industry vertical0-10 pts
  • Job title/seniority0-10 pts
  • Geography0-5 pts
  • Tech stack match0-10 pts

2 Engagement Score (50 points)

What they do. Behavioral signals indicating purchase intent.

  • Demo request+25 pts
  • Pricing page visit+15 pts
  • Case study download+10 pts
  • Webinar attendance+8 pts
  • Multiple blog visits+5 pts
  • Email engagement+1-3 pts

Step 3: Implement Score Decay

Engagement signals decay over time. A pricing page visit from 6 months ago doesn't indicate current intent. Apply time-based degradation to engagement scores only (ICP fit doesn't change).

Time Since Action Score Retention Example
0-30 days 100% Demo request worth full +25 pts
31-60 days 75% Demo request worth +19 pts
61-90 days 50% Demo request worth +13 pts
90+ days 25% Demo request worth +6 pts

Run decay calculations weekly via workflow automation. Reset decay clock when new high-intent action occurs.

Step 4: Set MQL Threshold

The 42 MQL Formula

MQL = (ICP_Fit_Score >= 25) AND (Total_Score >= 60)

Requiring minimum ICP fit (25/50) prevents high-engagement but poor-fit leads from qualifying. A marketing intern who downloads everything shouldn't become an MQL.

Too Few MQLs?

Lower threshold to 50 points, or improve top-of-funnel to increase engagement opportunities.

Too Many MQLs?

Raise threshold to 70+ points, or increase minimum ICP fit requirement to 30.

Low Conversion?

Increase weight on ICP fit criteria. Quality issues usually stem from fit, not engagement.

Step 5: Calibrate Against Historical Data

Don't launch on assumptions. Apply your model retroactively to validate it predicts actual outcomes.

Calibration Process

  1. 1 Export 6-12 months of leads with conversion outcomes (MQL → Opp → Won/Lost)
  2. 2 Apply your scoring formula in a spreadsheet to calculate scores at time of MQL
  3. 3 Compare average scores: Closed-Won vs Closed-Lost vs No Decision
  4. 4 Adjust weights until model correctly ranks 80%+ of wins above losses
  5. 5 Re-calibrate quarterly as ICP and buying patterns evolve

Frequently Asked Questions

What is lead scoring in B2B?

Lead scoring ranks leads by conversion likelihood. Effective B2B scoring combines ICP fit (firmographic match) and engagement (behavioral intent signals). The 42 Model uses a 100-point scale split evenly between these dimensions.

How do I build a lead scoring model?

Five steps: (1) Define ICP fit criteria from closed-won analysis, (2) Map engagement signals weighted by intent, (3) Implement score decay, (4) Set MQL threshold for 15-25 qualified leads per rep per week, (5) Calibrate against historical conversion data.

What is a good MQL score threshold?

A good threshold produces 15-25 leads per rep per week with 20%+ lead-to-opportunity conversion. Most B2B companies use 60-70 points on a 100-point scale. Require minimum ICP fit score to prevent poor-fit leads from qualifying on engagement alone.
